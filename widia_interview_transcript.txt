Hi, this is Vidya speaking. I'm currently working in the hospitality and private boat industry in Bali at a company called Blue, where I focus mainly on guest experience and frontline operations. In my daily role, I handle guests directly from the moment they arrive during check-in until they leave for the trip. I'm involved in making sure everything runs smoothly and guests feel well taken care of.


What I really enjoy about my role is that every day is different because we deal with different types of guests with varying expectations and sometimes unpredictable situations due to mother nature. That unpredictability is what makes hospitality interesting — how we stay calm with every guest request or complaint and adapt to still create a good experience no matter what.


A typical working day starts with checking team preparation, including the lounge condition, making sure it's clean, well organized, and ready to welcome guests. I coordinate with the operations team to confirm the boat schedule, amenities, and inclusions, and I check extras bookings like drinks, cheese platters, breakfast platters, cakes, or flowers for couples. We handle around 30 to 60 guests per day in low to mid season, and about double that during peak season.


The busiest mornings are around 7:30 when standard shared tours arrive, 8:00–8:15 for premium shared tours, and 8:30–9:30 for private and private premium bookings. During peak time, my focus is to ensure the flow is smooth from welcoming to check-in, answering guest questions, and coordinating with the team. After the departure window things relax a bit, but we stay alert for ongoing communications, last-minute changes, or special requests.


In the afternoon we prepare for returning guests, which is an important moment because it's the last impression. I ensure cold towels (oshibori), refreshments like iced tea and infused water, and fresh towels are ready. I also chat with guests about their trip to see if they were happy with the experience. I prefer to stay present on the floor to observe, approach guests when needed, and support the team because small things like greeting guests and responding quickly impact overall experience.


When I first started at Blue, the most common complaint was that the boats did not meet guest expectations. Often guests did not realize what they booked or what the boat looked like, which makes it hard to explain inclusions and manage expectations. Another common issue is guests misunderstanding whether they booked a private or shared tour. Weather-related cancellations or delays are also frequent; some guests understand, but others expect notification a day before and question why they were allowed to check in when rain started the night before. Since we deal with mother nature, I try my best to explain the situation and keep guests informed.


When handling complaints, my first approach is to stay calm and listen so guests feel heard. I acknowledge their concern, show empathy, explain the situation honestly, and offer a solution such as rescheduling, adjusting plans, or making them comfortable in the lounge. Communication is key — even if we can't fully solve the issue, how we communicate can change how guests feel.


I view repeated complaints as opportunities to improve. I take notes and discuss recurring issues with the operations team, and escalate them to our weekly or biweekly meetings to find preventive solutions. For example, if delays happen often during peak season when our harbor gets crowded, we should communicate the possibility of delays earlier.


The check-in process creates the first impression. It starts with welcoming guests and verifying booking vouchers, confirming booking types and balance payments, and explaining included amenities like towels and snorkeling equipment. We also brief guests about the trip using the wall map and provide identification like wristbands or lanyards to ease boarding. The process is fairly smooth but could be improved with automation; currently we print from our CRM and do manual entries.


For daily coordination I use WhatsApp because it's fast and practical, especially with captains and crew. For urgent situations we switch to phone calls or direct communication. I handle operational issues directly when possible to keep processes quick, but escalate major schedule changes, serious complaints, or compensation issues to my superior. Clear communication flow is important so everyone knows when to act or escalate.


Communication with Alex, Dima, Karim, David, and G is fast and smooth; I contact them by text or call depending on the issue. The biggest challenge recently has been consecutive cancellations due to strong winds and heavy rain. Our focus then is communication and guest handling: explaining the situation clearly, giving regular updates, and making guests feel cared for while hoping the trip can still proceed.


From that experience, we improved pre-trip information to set better expectations. A simple sentence at check-in when it’s raining — telling guests we are waiting on harbor master updates and that we will keep them updated — has reduced complaints about delayed or canceled trips.


Regarding opening a new lounge, the most important things are a clear and simple system, a complete setup checklist, thorough training so everyone shares the same service standard, and careful guest journey design from arrival to return. The goal is for guests to feel everything is smooth, easy, and professional even at a new location.


Overall, my strength is balancing operations and guest experience. I'm hands-on and enjoy being directly involved. I focus on small improvements because they can create big differences in guest satisfaction, and I'm always open to learning and adapting in this fast-moving hospitality environment.
